Research Question

Can Spotify popularity be predicted from sound and metadata?

We predict each track's numeric Spotify popularity score from audio characteristics and lightweight metadata, then analyze which signals contribute most to prediction.

114,000tracks
114genres

Method

Grouped evaluation, strong baselines, and ablation study.

Feature Design

The model uses Spotify audio features such as danceability, energy, acousticness, valence, loudness, and tempo, plus metadata such as genre, duration, and explicit status.

Raw identifiers and high-cardinality text fields are excluded to reduce memorization of individual tracks or artists.

Models Compared

The train/test split is grouped by track_id, so the same track never appears in both sets.

Key Findings

Popularity is not purely an acoustic property.

Genre is the strongest signal. track_genre has the largest permutation importance, suggesting market context matters.
Audio features help, but only partially. Audio-only models are useful but weaker than models with metadata.
High-popularity songs are hard. The model often underpredicts hits, likely because artist fame and playlist exposure are missing.
Grouped splitting improves evaluation. Splitting by track_id avoids near-duplicate leakage across train and test.

Conclusion

Useful signal, but missing context remains important.

Spotify popularity is only partially predictable from audio content. Genre and metadata provide important context, but popularity is not purely a musical or acoustic property. External factors such as artist fame, playlists, marketing, release timing, and social trends remain important missing signals.
